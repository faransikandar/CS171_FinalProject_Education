WHAT DETERMINES ENROLLMENT IN HIGHER EDUCATION IN A COUNTRY?

There may be many factors that influence whether an individual in a given country enrolls in higher education or not. The matrix below looks at the relative effect of 11 such factors using nationally representative survey data in each country.

Hover over the cells for more detail. Use the variable and country labels to sort the cells.
In many developing countries, costs -- either direct educational costs or opportunity costs from foregone earnings -- are often issues that prevent children from attending school. The matrix shows that being poor (being in the bottom 40% of the country's wealth distribution) is the single most defining factor in whether an individual obtains higher education or not.
Parent and community education seem to play an important role in influencing enrollment in many countries in the sample. Higher educational attainment may reflect greater value placed on education within the household or community, which is likely to affect whether or not a child attends school.

So...what's it like to be in another's shoes?

Through this website, hopefully, you've received a glimpe of what your educational outcomes may have been like if you were born in another country. You have seen:

  1. How educational outcomes vary geographically
  2. How average educational attainment have varied over time
  3. How educational attainment is affected according to different subsets of the population, according to gender, rural/urban, and income quintiles
  4. What factors most affect enrollment in any given country

While the outcomes vary drastically across countries, we can discern some fairly consistent patterns across region and segments of the population. For example, where Africa has lagged behind in terms of average educational attainment, Europe has made large gains. Over time, the educational disparity gap between the least well educated and most well educated (in terms of years of attainment) has actually increased since the 1950s. More, we see that girls don't have as much education as boys, poorer people don't have as much education as richer people, and rural people don't have as much education as the urban. There area myriad of factors to consider in this analysis, which were out of scope for this website, including:

  • Age of entering or exiting schooling
  • Education quality
  • Actual learning levels
  • Incentives to pursue education vs. work
  • Migration patterns (especially for elite in developing countries to pursue education abroad)
  • School capacity (e.g. teacher quality, funding, supplies, etc.)

Of course, these facts might seem obvious, but did you notice (e.g. on the line graphs) certain trends in the steep drop-off in educational outcome after a certain grade level? This points to the fact that there are key inflection points in someone's education that may drastically change his/her life trajectory. By studying the circumstances and systemic factors around these inflection points (e.g. by isolating and studying individual factors that may affect a specific outcome, such as enrollment, as we illustrated on the matrix plot in the last visualization), policy makers and civil society can work together to positively affect the individual factors which may have negative influence on a person's educational path.
